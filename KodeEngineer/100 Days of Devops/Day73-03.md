It is completely understandable why you felt stuck! In many DevOps lab scenarios, certain "prerequisite" configurations—like setting up plugins or SSH keys—are often expected as part of the environment setup, even if they aren't explicitly listed in the step-by-step task requirements.

### Why he used the 'Publish Over SSH' plugin
At **4:32**, he identified that the goal was to copy logs from an *App Server* to a *Storage Server* using a *Jenkins job*. Since Jenkins runs on its own server, it needs a way to talk to these remote servers securely. 

*   **What the plugin does:** The **Publish Over SSH** plugin allows Jenkins to connect to remote servers via SSH, execute shell commands, and transfer files. Without it, Jenkins would have no built-in way to log into those other servers to perform the `scp` (secure copy) operations needed to move the logs.
*   **How he knew:** Experienced engineers recognize this as a common pattern in Jenkins. When you see a task that involves "centralizing" files or running commands on multiple remote servers, the standard solution is to provide Jenkins with the credentials and connectivity tools to reach those machines.

### How to improve your Jenkins skills
To avoid getting stuck on these "hidden" steps in the future, consider these strategies:

1.  **Understand the Architecture:** Before starting the task, draw a quick mental or physical map (like the one he mentions at **0:30**). Ask yourself: *"Where is the data? Where does it need to go? What tool (Jenkins) is controlling this?"* If the tool needs to touch a remote server, it will almost always need a plugin or SSH configuration.
2.  **Review Common Plugins:** Familiarize yourself with the 'staple' Jenkins plugins. *Publish Over SSH*, *Git*, and *Pipeline* are the "Big Three" you will see in almost every automation lab.
3.  **Don't Fear the Console Output:** If a job fails, always look at the **Console Output** in Jenkins (as he does at **20:35**). It will often give you a specific error (like "Permission denied" or "Command not found") that points you toward what configuration is missing.
4.  **Practice "Environment-First" Setup:** In many real-world labs, your first step should be to ensure Jenkins can successfully talk to your target nodes. Try creating a simple job that just runs `uptime` or `ls` on the remote server before you try to implement the actual logic.

For more on how to manage remote servers with Jenkins, I recommend checking out official Jenkins documentation or community tutorials on "Jenkins Remote Execution."
